Severity concerns

Based on (Fig. 1), the domains with the highest tallied severity points were:

  • Documentation/Tutorial
  • Game play balance
  • Immersion

Documentation/Tutorial

Documentation/Tutorial has overall collected the most severity points, primarily because the game provides no information on how to play or change characters (Fig. 1). This is noteworthy as an issue that could not have been discovered in the player evaluation, as that sample was introduced to the controls beforehand. The introduction of a tutorial in the game could help to mitigate confusion in the case that controls are not provided.

This could be mitigated by adding a tutorial to the early part of the game.

The controls and abilities, in turn, can be displayed at any time when the player taps the F1 key.

Game play balance

Game play balance tied with Immersion for the most severity points. Three of the respondents confidently describe a difficulty killing the pigeon. However, one of them acknowledges that the pigeon's drawbacks make them feel not particularly overpowered (Fig. 1). This could be because the pigeon, indeed, kills itself as a core mechanic.

As the results are unanimous, it is unreasonable to deny the difficulty in hitting a pigeon. An immediate solution may be to make the pigeon easier to hit, perhaps by increasing their hitbox, or making their TNT react to nearby electrical projectiles from the bunny.

However, a (questionably-entitled) designer's perspective informs us that this may not strictly be an issue of balance in overall gameplay, but an issue of balance in the ability for the characters to hit each other. If, for example, it were made harder for pigeons to hit the bunnies, it could be a boost to heuristic B (variety of play styles), as players playing as the pigeon are allowed a more free-flowing, conflict-free gameplay that regardless grants them occasional opportunities to destroy bunnies. Further design discussions and player research could be conducted to ascertain the necessity of this change.

An alternative, longer-term solution would be to add a new character that can counter the pigeon whilst not overpowering the bunny. An example of such a character that could be revived is the Tank Tortoise, a land-based, slow animal that is too slow to easily escape the bunnies, but has surface-to-air projectiles that can track and destroy pigeons. This could give the pigeon a viable threat. Implementing this character is a long-term goal, but not achievable in an adequately short time scale.

Immersion

The primary threat to immersion is the lack sound and music. This has been noted with severities of 5 and 4 in heuristic F (immersion) and a further 5 and 3 in heuristic I (game feedback) (Fig. 1), making the audio a high-scoring issue overall (total = 17).

The simplest yet most resource-intensive solution is to create and add sounds and music into the game. Noting heuristic I's responses, sound effects could take precedence in order to provide additional player feedback, and music could follow to improve the immersion.

Player Evaluation

Context

This evaluation was carried out on a small sample of players (N = 8), who had never played the game before. This took place in the games academy studio at Falmouth University. The small sample size made the interpretation of qualitative feedback manageable and brief; however, all data has been assumed as only preliminarily reliable, as the size of the sample is small enough for the margin of error to cover a wide area.

Frustration vs Control

One pitfall of the pigeon is an increased frustration with the character compared to the bunny as evidenced in (Fig 2). However, while the pigeon was reported to be easier to control, it was also reported to be significantly more frustrating to the player (Fig. 2). This could contradict an assumption that the frustration is due to difficult controls.

Furthermore, despite the significantly higher reports of player frustration, the other attributes performed quite similarly between the pigeon and the bunny. That suggests that despite the allegedly more frustrating experience, the pigeon was still highly enjoyable to play.

Where is the frustration coming from?

The Homing Pigeon's primary means of attacking players is by crashing into them, with the pigeon exploding and dying in the process to fulfill its noble desTNTny. The following unfavourable responses in (Fig. 3) are therefore considered: dying, killing yourself as the bir[d], and attacking. It would stand to reason that although it is the destiny of the pigeon to explode on impact, this process of dying in a self-inflicted manner could be frustrating to players, especially if they feel that the spawning time is too high, or that it is difficult to aim and hard to hit other players (Fig. 3).

In the case of the Homing Pigeon, the main two challenges appear to be in aiming and dying. As exploding and dying is a fundamental part of the Homing Pigeon's noble destiny, the future development of the Homing Pigeon could instead dedicate toward improving the aiming process, and perhaps the spawn time. The heuristic evaluation could provide more insight on how the aiming process should be improved.

Character preferences

Fig. 4: Comparison of player character preferences based on certain traits

The player's preferences for each character are roughly consistent with the findings in the previous section. Even the aesthetic of the bunny character was preferred. However, the pigeon performed more strongly in a hypothetical, idealised gameplay scenario wherein the character is designed perfectly. It is thus likely that, should the pigeon be easier to control, more powerful, or simply less frustrating, it could be an equally popular character. This creates a strong argument for improving this character in particular.

Bias in aesthetic

Fig. 5: Comparison of player character aesthetic preferences with gameplay preferences

A question raised during the assessment of the results was whether the aesthetic of a character could influence the player's preferences for the gameplay of that character. As illustrated above, there seems to be no positive correlation between a player's preference for a character's aesthetics and their gameplay. In fact, amusingly, players who preferred the aesthetic design of the Pigeon (38%) actually preferred the Bunny's gameplay, and even then, they appeared to enjoy the overall experience less. Contrastingly, players who preferred the aesthetic of the Bunny enjoyed both characters more.

The sample size is arguably too small to derive a definitive answer. However, one hypothesis is that those who enjoyed the aesthetics of the pigeon played it more. As reported above, the pigeon character was overall less enjoyable to play. Therefore, the correlation could be a result of recall bias created from the reduced overall enjoyability experienced by these players as a result of playing the pigeon more frequently. This may have influenced their responses to both characters.

For further research, the video footage of the players could be analysed to find the playtime of each character by the respondents. If a link exists between the aesthetic preferences of a character and the time spent playing that character, this could add further plausibility to the above hypothesis.

Ideal number of players

Fig. 5: Population of players who desired less (1) or more (5) players.

Where 5 was described as "I want to see more players", whilst 1 was described as "I want to see less players", it is noted that all players in this sample wanted to see either more players in the game, or were neutral. The mean indicates only a marginal interest, although some outlying respondents were significantly certain that they wanted more players.

There were eight players total in the gameplay session. The results (Fig. 5) suggest that the number of players could be increased for better enjoyability. This, however, is likely to be sensitive to factors such as the version of the game, number of characters available, maps and modes. Regardless, should the core gameplay remain relatively unchanged for this evaluation, a future evaluation could increase the player count. The amount by which to increase it is uncertain, as the question was deliberately non-specific; but it could be recursively tested until the player count which best satisfies the centre of this Likert scale is found: this will reveal the most popular player counts.

It is marginally possible that this question was misinterpreted by some for an opinion on the overall popularity of the game. However, since the question, "How did you feel about the number of players?", was asked in past tense (indicating its applicability to the actual play session), this is unlikely.
